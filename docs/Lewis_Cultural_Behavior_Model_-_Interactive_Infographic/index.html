üíº Why They Clash at Work

1

Linear Presenting to Multi-Active

The Problem: You've prepared a beautiful agenda. The Multi-active doesn't care‚Äîthey want to build relationship first.

Why: Your agenda means nothing until there's a personal connection.

üìå Tip: Start with personal chat, build trust, THEN discuss business.

2

Multi-Active in a Linear Meeting

The Problem: You feel suffocated. The Linear person feels like the room is on fire.

Why: Different engagement styles‚Äîwhat's expressive to you is chaotic to them.

üìå Tip: Adapt your energy; respect their need for structure.

3

Reactive Won't Say "No"

The Problem: You ask for feedback. They stop responding. Silence IS disagreement.

Why: Direct refusal causes loss of face and disrupts harmony.

üìå Tip: Ask indirectly; look for non-verbal cues; never force a public "no".

4

German √ó Italian Clash

The Problem: German directness offends Italian relationship-focus. Italian emotion frustrates German logic.

Why: Different values‚Äîefficiency vs. connection.

üìå Tip: Germans soften directness; Italians accept efficiency.

5

Dutch Directness √ó Arab Sensitivity

The Problem: Dutch straight talk feels disrespectful to Arab colleagues who value politeness and face-saving.

Why: Different communication thresholds.

üìå Tip: Dutch add diplomacy; Arabs appreciate directness if respectful.

6

Different Culture, Same Company

The Problem: What works brilliantly in one room dies in another.

Why: No single "best" communication style‚Äîit's about cultural fit.

üìå Tip: Understand your team's makeup; adapt your leadership style.

üí° Practical Tips for Working Across Cultures

ü§ù

With Linear Colleagues

  • Stick to agenda & timelines
  • Be direct & factual
  • Prepare documents in advance
  • Respect their planning approach
  • Avoid last-minute changes
üéâ

With Multi-Active Colleagues

  • Allow flexibility in schedule

  • Engage with emotion, not just logic
üéß

With Reactive Colleagues

  • Never force a public answer
  • Respect silence & reflection
  • Protect their face/honor
  • Build long-term relationships

Universal Best Practice

  • Ask about preferences
  • Adapt your style
  • Show cultural curiosity
  • Default to respect
