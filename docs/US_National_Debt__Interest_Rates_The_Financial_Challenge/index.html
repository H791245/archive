Treasury Debt Maturity Strategy
  • Bills (< 1 year): ~22% of debt. Easy to manage, but refinanced frequently at current rates.
  • Notes (2-10 years): Largest portion. Issued years ago at low rates‚Äînow maturing and being refinanced at higher rates. This is the pain point.
  • Bonds (10+ years): Smaller portion. Locked in long-term rates, less immediate pressure.
  • Strategy: When rates were low (2008, 2020), government issued longer-term debt to lock in rates and extend maturity.
  • Current Issue: With higher rates, government is reducing issuance of long-term bonds to avoid locking in high rates.

The "Great Melt-Up" Scenario
  • Housing Costs: Already inflated significantly over past 3 years
  • Stock Markets: Valuations rising, often disconnected from economic fundamentals
  • Precious Metals: Gold and silver prices rising as inflation hedge
  • Healthcare & Groceries: Noticeably more expensive; cost-of-living crisis for consumers
  • What is "Melt-Up"? Not meltdown‚Äîassets rise in nominal value, but purchasing power falls. You feel wealthier on paper, but inflation erodes actual wealth.
The Risk: Hyperinflation
Current inflation is a "melt-up." Theory: If government prints massive money to fund itself while debt balloons, we could enter true hyperinflation followed by a "great reset" of the financial system.

  • Financial System Stress: Banking crisis, credit crunch, or liquidity crisis
